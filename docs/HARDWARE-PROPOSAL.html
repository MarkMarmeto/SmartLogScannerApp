Executive Summary

We propose a fully hands-free, self-service hardware bundle for the SmartLog Scanner attendance station at each school gate. Students present their QR code directly to the scanning station — no staff operator is required, eliminating ongoing personnel cost and removing the queue bottleneck of human-mediated scanning.

The bundle combines two fixed webcams (the primary scan path, with on-screen visual feedback) and one fixed presentation scanner (a kiosk-style unit for fast self-service scanning), driven by a dedicated mini-PC running the SmartLog Scanner application.

Technical Justification

Why two webcams (not one, not three)

One camera is a single point of failure — if it fails or is obstructed, the gate stops accepting scans. Two cameras provide:

  • Redundancy: if one camera fails, the second continues to operate without interruption.
  • Parallel decode lanes: two students can present QR codes simultaneously, doubling throughput at peak arrival times.
  • Cost-effectiveness: Logitech C920 provides commercial-grade optics at ~₱3,500/unit. Two units total ~₱7,000 — significantly cheaper than a single industrial-grade fixed scanner.

Three or more cameras provide diminishing returns: the limiting factor at a gate is student arrival rate and physical queueing, not decode throughput. Two cameras handle 50+ students/min comfortably, which exceeds the peak load of most schools.

Why a presentation scanner in addition to cameras

A fixed presentation scanner adds value as a complementary scan path, not a replacement for cameras:

  • Familiar UX: students recognise the form factor from supermarket self-checkout — intuitive, no training needed.
  • Fast decode: presentation scanners provide a bright red illumination spot and audible beep, giving immediate unambiguous confirmation.
  • Better in low light: active illumination performs reliably even in shaded gate areas.
  • Reads damaged QR codes: scratched or smudged ID cards that cameras may struggle with are easily handled by the active illumination of a presentation scanner.

Why a dedicated mini-PC (not a laptop or shared workstation)

  • Sustained CPU performance: the multi-camera decode pipeline is a continuous CPU workload. Laptops thermally throttle within 10–15 minutes of full load; mini-PCs maintain rated performance indefinitely.
  • Sufficient USB connectivity: mini-PCs typically expose 4–6 USB ports across multiple controllers, enough for 2 cameras + 1 scanner + peripherals without contention.
  • Form factor: mini-PCs mount discreetly behind the display via VESA brackets, leaving the gate counter clear.
  • Cost parity: a mid-range mini-PC is cheaper than a comparable-CPU laptop and avoids paying for screen, battery, and keyboard that are not needed.

Why a UPS is non-negotiable

The application maintains a local SQLite queue of pending scan submissions. Sudden power loss mid-scan can corrupt this database, requiring manual recovery and risking lost attendance records. A small 650 VA UPS provides a 5–10 minute window for the application to flush its queue and shut down cleanly. This is the highest-value low-cost accessory in the bundle.

Why hands-free / self-service

  • No recurring labour cost: a staff operator at each gate represents an ongoing salary cost; hands-free hardware is a one-time capital expense.
  • Higher throughput: students self-presenting QR codes is faster than a single operator scanning each one in sequence.
  • Operates outside duty hours: early arrivals or late dismissals require no staffing.
  • Consistent enforcement: the system applies the same validation logic to every scan, avoiding human inconsistency.

Risk Mitigation
Risk Mitigation built into this proposal
Single device failure stops the gate Two independent scan paths (cameras + presentation scanner). Either can serve students if the other fails.
Power outage corrupts the local database UPS included as standard. Application performs graceful shutdown with queue flush.
Network outage prevents submission to backend Application includes built-in offline queue with automatic background sync when connectivity is restored. No scans are lost.
Hardware obsolescence All proposed components are commodity standards (UVC webcams, USB HID scanners, x86 mini-PC). Replacement parts will be available throughout the 5-year deployment lifecycle.
Insufficient throughput at peak times Configuration supports 50–70 scans/min per gate, which exceeds the peak rate of typical school populations. Capacity can be expanded by adding a second gate (linear scaling).
Student unfamiliarity with self-service On-screen visual feedback through the camera preview guides students; printed signage at the gate provides instructions for first-time use. Two scan paths reduce reliance on any single interaction model.

Implementation Notes

Procurement & lead time

  • All proposed components are available from local distributors (Lazada, Shopee Mall, PC Express, DataBlitz, retail electronics).
  • Estimated procurement lead time: 1–2 weeks for stock items.
  • Consolidated procurement of multiple gate bundles may yield ~5–10% volume discount from a single distributor.

Installation

  • Per-gate physical installation: approximately 4 hours (mounting, cabling, network connection, initial application configuration).
  • Network switch placement: install in the school server room or network closet alongside the SmartLog backend server. Single uplink cable connects the switch to the existing school router/gateway.
  • Installation can be performed by school IT staff with the deployment guide provided in deploy/.
  • Each gate requires: 1× standard 220V power outlet, 1× Cat6 Ethernet run from gate location to the network switch, gate-side counter or wall-mount surface.

Warranty & lifecycle

  • Standard manufacturer warranty applies to each component (Logitech: 2 years, mini-PC: 1–3 years brand-dependent, UPS: 2 years).
  • Expected operational lifecycle: 5 years before recommended hardware refresh.
  • Annual maintenance budget allowance recommended: ~5% of capital cost (~₱2,800/gate/year) for replacement cables, occasional camera replacement, UPS battery refresh.

Training & rollout

  • No specialised training required for students — the system is designed for self-service.
  • School IT staff training: ~2 hours for installation, configuration, and routine maintenance procedures.
  • Recommended phased rollout: pilot one gate for 2 weeks, validate throughput and student adoption, then deploy remaining gates.
